Historical Highlights

Pioneering voices that defined their eras and shaped public discourse.

1880s 01

The Sentinel

Trenton, NJ • 1880–1882

Trenton's first Black newspaper, founded by R. Henri Herbert. A pioneering voice in the post-Reconstruction era, published weekly at 4 North Broad Street. Merged with the Trenton Herald in 1883.

1880s 02

New Jersey Trumpet

Jersey City / Newark • 1887–1897

Founded by William Murrell, this weekly advocated for civil rights, economic advancement, and political participation during rapid urban growth and discrimination.

1900s 03

The Echo

Long Branch / Red Bank • 1904–1943

"The oldest colored paper in New Jersey." Served the Jersey Shore's Black communities for nearly four decades during the Jim Crow era.

1930s 04

New Jersey Guardian

Newark, NJ • 1934–1942

"Voice of the Colored People." Represented Newark's Black community during the Great Depression and early World War II, edited by F. R. Clark.

1940s 05

NJ Afro-American

Newark, NJ • 1941–Present

The longest-running Black newspaper in New Jersey, operating continuously for over 80 years. A major voice during the Civil Rights era.

1960s 06

Black Newark

Newark, NJ • 1968–1974

"The Voice of New Ark's Inner City." Emerged during intense political organizing following the 1967 Newark uprising, published by the Committee for Unified Newark.

About the project

The New Jersey Black Press Database is a digital preservation initiative by the Center for Cooperative Media, dedicated to documenting the history and ongoing contributions of Black-owned news publications throughout the Garden State.

From early 19th-century abolitionist newspapers to contemporary digital outlets, the Black Press has documented community life, challenged discrimination, and amplified voices often overlooked by mainstream media. New Jersey’s proximity to New York and Philadelphia shaped a distinct press tradition rooted in local advocacy, civic participation, and cultural preservation.

This project highlights major publications, influential editors, and the themes that defined coverage — civil rights reporting, local news, political engagement, and cultural arts — while also reflecting on the policy landscape that has shaped Black-owned media, from historic barriers to today’s digital and regulatory challenges.

By preserving these archives and stories, the database underscores the Black Press’s enduring role in informing and empowering New Jersey’s Black communities, and it supports ongoing efforts to sustain this vital media legacy.

Key initiatives

Beyond archiving, this project supports the Black Publishers Collective (BPC), a network fostering collaboration and sustainability among Black media creators.

It also aligns with the South Jersey Information Equity Project (SJIEP), which trains aspiring community reporters to enhance local news coverage and civic resilience in underserved areas.
